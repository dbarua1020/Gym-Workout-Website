The Ultimate Guide to Ab Training: Unveiling Your Core



The answer WILL surprise you.



BY - TY THOMAS
MARCH 8, 2024




What's up folks? It's your boy Coach Ty, and today we've got our ab body part guide. As usual, we'll dive into anatomy, volume recommendations, and my favorite exercises. So let's get straight to it.



Understanding the Anatomy of Abs:


Before we jump into the nitty-gritty of ab training, it's essential to understand the anatomy of the abdominal muscles. Specifically, we're focusing on the rectus abdominis, the muscle responsible for that coveted six-pack look. The rectus abdominis consists of two flat parallel muscles separated by the linea alba, a connective tissue.
One crucial point to clarify is that everyone already has abs! They are an inherent part of the human anatomy. The key to achieving that defined, chiseled look is reducing body fat percentage. Abs are hidden beneath a layer of fat, so before embarking on extensive ab training, focus on lowering your body fat percentage.


The Function of the Rectus Abdominis:


The rectus abdominis primarily functions in flexion of the trunk, essentially rounding your spine. While it's generally advisable to avoid excessive spine flexion, there are specific exercises where it's acceptable, like crunches or sit-ups. Another misconception is that crunches are harmful to your spine. Research suggests that they are not detrimental under controlled conditions. Additionally, the rectus abdominis plays a vital role in tensing the anterior wall of the abdomen against pressure or resistance, aiding in compressing the contents of the abdomen. This unique feature sets it apart from other muscles. It also helps with posterior pelvic tilt and contributes to core stability, acting as a natural weight belt to protect your lower back.


Volume Recommendations:


When it comes to ab training volume, there are two main categories: resisting movement or stabilization (planks) and trunk flexion (crunches and leg raises).
Plank Category:
  • i) Duration: Aim for 20 to 60 seconds per set
  • ii) Progression: Start with forearm planks and advance to hand planks. Add resistance by placing weights on your back or incorporating advanced variations like long lever planks

Trunk Flexion Category:
  • i) Repetitions: Target 12 to 15 reps per set.
  • ii) Exercise Selection: Rotate between crunch variations (upper abs) and leg raise variations (lower abs). Choose based on your specific goals and preferences.

Exercise Selection:

Here are some exercises to consider within the two categories:

Plank Category:

i) Forearm Plank
ii) Hand Plank
iii) Long Lever Forearm Plank
iv) Long Lever Hand Plank

Trunk Flexion Category (Crunch Variations):
i) BOSU Crunch
ii) Knee Tuck
iii) Leg Raise
iv) Toes to Bar
